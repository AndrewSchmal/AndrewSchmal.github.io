Grocery Shopping Without a List

One of the first steps to becoming a great home cook is mastering your grocery shopping habits. If you've tried learning to cook before by following recipes and that hasn't worked for you, I'd recommend focusing on meal building blocks. This way, you can create flexible meals with the ingredients you have, reducing food waste and unnecessary costs. Isn't it the worst buying an ingredient specifically for one recipe and then worrying about the rest of it going bad?

Mastering Meal Prep

Meal prep is the art of preparing meals or individual ingredients ahead of time, making it easier to enjoy healthy and efficient meals during your busy days. Below are different ways to meal prep, so you can pick what fits your lifestyle the best!

Choose one specific day of the week to do all your meal prep for extra efficiency. Most people choose Saturday or Sunday so they don't have to cook during a weeknight and can prepare all their meals for the month, but do whatever works best for you. I personally prefer to do reheat meals, freezing extras so I don't have to worry about freshness by the time I get to them.

Pantry Essentials for Every Home Cook

Stocking your pantry with the right ingredients ensures you’re always ready to whip up a meal, even on busy days. Baking vs Cooking: Structured vs Creative

Baking and cooking are very similar, however there are a few general differences I'd like to point out. I think both are rewarding and worth learning, but it's good to keep in mind the slightly different approaches. An analogy I've heard pretty often is that cooking is an art, while baking is a science.

Baking

  • Precision is key: Follow the recipe exactly or risk disaster!
  • Science-based: Baking is chemistry, involving specific reactions with ingredients.
  • Patience required: Once it’s in the oven, all you can do is wait.
  • Great for sweet treats: Cakes, cookies, pastries, and bread.
  • Planning-heavy: Ingredients are usually measured to the gram.
  • Less forgiving: It's harder to "save" something you messed up. If it's baked it's baked. You can't unbake your bread to add the flour you forgot.

Cooking

  • Room for creativity: Improvise and adjust flavors as you go.
  • Heat and timing: It’s all about knowing when and how to use heat.
  • Quick or slow: Meals can be fast ("weeknight meals") or take hours to prepare (Holiday dinners).
  • Endless variety: Soups, stir-fries, pastas, and more.
  • Flexible measurements: You can eyeball most ingredients without disaster once you have the basics down.
  • Easier to recover from mistakes: Easier to "save" something you messed up. Gravy too thin? Add flour. Forgot something in a stew? You can improvise.

Both baking and cooking have their own charm—whether you enjoy following a strict recipe or going with the flow in the kitchen, mastering both skills will elevate your culinary game. Why not try a bit of both and see what you enjoy most?
